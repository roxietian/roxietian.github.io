16 · STAR — AI Hotline

Hook → S/T → Action×4 → Result → Aha → Lesson
Hook
The hardest part of rolling out AI across seven markets wasn't building the AI → my own local teams were quietly refusing to let it go live → and honestly, they had a point
S/T
I led the rollout of a multilingual AI hotline across seven markets → teams manually screened repetitive questions, over 1,000 requests per week → the problem was not only speed, it was scalability
Act 1
I sat down with the teams to understand what held them back → two concerns: validation felt like extra work, and who's responsible if AI is wrong
Act 2
First, I clarified the AI use-case boundary → only L1 and knowledge access, never repair, liability, SLA, contract, or case closure
Act 3
Second, I worked with R&D to map the full reporting workflow → if it can't answer or the user wants human help, it creates a Salesforce ticket into our O&M workflow
Act 4
Third, validation was slow, so I started with three pilot markets → by language, willingness, customer importance → I reframed the value as reducing their own L1 workload, and escalated to senior leaders for resources
Result
After the first three saw the value, the others joined → all seven markets, over a hundred clients, more than fifty calls a day at peak, about fifty percent automated → plus coverage outside business hours
Aha
My aha moment → the hardest part wasn't building the AI, it was designing trust → people needed to know what AI could and couldn't do, and who remained accountable
Lesson
First, change leadership starts with understanding why people resist, then making the value real, not pushing harder → Second, adoption is a trust problem → trust comes from clear boundaries and human accountability, not a better model

17 · STAR — 0-1 Service Ecosystem

Hook → S/T → Aha → Action → Result → Lesson
Hook
A zero-to-one story, but the twist is my first client almost broke my own design → I built a CRM-based service ecosystem from scratch, no benchmark → first client was Shell, and to hit their deadline we shipped a Shell-specific version → then change requests came flooding in
S/T
The ecosystem was five connected platforms → every client had a different SLA model, packages, permission structure, reporting → my job was to make sure the first client could scale to hundreds more
Aha
At first the requests looked like normal first-release noise BUT the pattern was → it had all been hardcoded to one client → the feedback meant the product model itself wouldn't scale
Action
So I decided to change direction → one standard core with a configurable rule layer → same core workflow, only the rules differ → engineering pushed back, so I used evidence, not a vision: Shell's cost, repeated RFP rules, the pipeline → and I made the build iterative, in phases
Result
Onboarding became a configuration task vs a new development project → grew to 200+ enterprise clients and over 3,000 users → killed repeated engineering, manual setup, one-offs → this model became the benchmark
Lesson
Feedback works on two levels → now I ask one question first → different process, or same process with different rules? → a rule you configure, a process you build → in zero-to-one, sometimes you build the benchmark

18 · STAR — Training Platform

Hook → S/T → Action×3 → Result → Aha → Lesson
Hook
We launched a training platform, the mandatory gate to our entire O&M service chain, no certification, no access BUT over a thousand technicians were already trained and paid offline → a re-pay would have triggered open resistance from our largest customers
S/T
I owned this platform from zero → its real job was the entry point to the whole service ecosystem → the hard part was resistance: they wouldn't re-pay or repeat training, and believed old certificates should count
Act 1
First, the migration → I didn't force a re-buy, I ran a free whitelist migration → a thousand migrated free, a one-time exception, new courses still paid → but that's a P&L decision for the CEO, so I brought him a clear recommendation
Act 2
Second, I scoped it beyond a learning system → invite-only, so it became a CRM and a service-governance asset → it opened revenue, around a million euros a year, and a feedback channel
Act 3
Third, my real mistake → I assumed the PMs would absorb the process on their own, they didn't → so I treated it as a capability problem → flowcharts, manuals, role-plays, scripts, co-onboarding, across ten PMs in six countries
Result
We reached 3,000+ certified users and ~€1M a year → the result I value most is that it ran without me → one PM even pushed sales to sell courses, and that turned into real revenue
Aha
My real misjudgment was assuming people would absorb the why on their own → a zero-to-one platform scales when the operators own the why, not just the how
Lesson
So now → I treat enabling the local owners as part of the build, not a handover at the end
